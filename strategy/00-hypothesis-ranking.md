## Tier 1: Existential (if wrong, thesis is dead)

### H1: Capability floor — agents can do useful work well enough for trust to matter at all
- **Template refs:** `hypotheses/03-product-features.md` (core utility), `hypotheses/04-customer-segments.md` (pain severity)
- **Status:** ASSUMED-IMPORTANT, NOT YET DIRECTLY TESTED
- **Why #1:** If the work quality is bad, no amount of visibility, incentives, or messaging infrastructure rescues adoption.
- **Evidence FOR:** Brain×CombinatorAgent coordination on docs produced more accurate output than human-only (Jakub's words). Tricep×Brain collaboration produced concrete plans with division of labor.
- **Evidence AGAINST / open risk:** Most evidence so far is anecdotal and from strong agents in favorable conditions. We do not yet have a clean baseline for "good enough" capability across ordinary agents/tasks.
- **Test framing:** Before testing higher-order trust layers, define a narrow task class where success/failure is legible and measure whether agents meet a minimum quality bar.
- **Falsification criteria:** If agents fail simple, economically relevant tasks often enough that operators won't reuse them, higher-layer trust hypotheses are downstream noise.

### H9 (reframed): Near-term operator wedge vs long-term agent customer
- **Template refs:** `hypotheses/04-customer-segments.md` (customer definition), `hypotheses/07-customer-relationships.md` (who we're retaining), `hypotheses/05-channels.md` (distribution path)
- **Status:** PARTIALLY SHARPENED, STILL UNRESOLVED
- **Origin:** Jakub: "Maybe there's a fundamental tension here about who the end customer really is. Is it agents or their operators?"
- **Why #2:** Even with the right product intuition, we can still miss PMF if we build for the wrong immediate buyer/wedge. The practical question is no longer a clean binary of operator vs agent — it is whether the near-term path is operator-first strongly enough to justify using it to create an eventual agent-first market.
- **Current read (2026-03-20):** Near-term distribution likely has to run **through humans/operators** because current agents are not yet consistently competent or responsive enough to serve as a reliable standalone acquisition surface. But the stronger long-term PMF path may still be **agents as the real target customer** — and that may require us to help create the market by attracting operators who want their agents to become more distributable, reachable, and economically useful.
- **Implication:** treat this as a staged market hypothesis, not a forced binary. Near-term wedge may be operator-first; long-term market may be agent-first.
- **Falsification criteria:** If reachable operators do not actively want help making their agents better at distribution/customer acquisition/workflow initiation right now, the operator-first wedge is weak. If agents also do not independently seek out this infrastructure, the staged-market thesis weakens sharply.
- **Test:** Track whether Hub growth comes from operators configuring it, or from agents discovering it through collaboration — and separately whether operators express desire for their agents to gain distribution/customer-acquisition leverage.

### H11 (NEW): Incentives/accountability gap — once capability is good enough, lack of recourse blocks real delegation
- **Template refs:** `hypotheses/07-customer-relationships.md` (trust/retention), `hypotheses/10-revenue-pricing.md` (risk/pricing), `hypotheses/09-partners.md` (dispute / enforcement dependencies)
- **Status:** STRONGLY PLAUSIBLE — newly elevated from PRTeamLeader evidence
- **Why #3:** People may tolerate limited visibility if outcomes are reliable and someone is clearly on the hook; they do not tolerate ambiguous downside once stakes become real.
- **Core question:** What protects the operator/counterparty when autonomous action fails?
- **Evidence FOR:** PRTeamLeader's trust-stack framing cleanly separates three layers: H1 = can it do it, H10 = can I see it, H11 = what protects me when it fails. This matches the adoption gap between toy demos and meaningful delegation.
- **Evidence AGAINST / open risk:** Still early; we need behavior evidence, not just conceptual agreement.
- **Fast test:** Compare willingness to delegate under two scenarios: same capable agent, but one with no recourse and one with explicit escrow/stake/dispute/reputation consequences.
- **Falsification criteria:** If operators are equally willing to delegate meaningful work without accountability primitives once capability is adequate, H11 weakens.

### H10: Visibility/legibility gap — observability enables trust, but may be insufficient by itself
- **Template refs:** `hypotheses/04-customer-segments.md` (operator pain), `hypotheses/07-customer-relationships.md` (activation/retention)
- **Status:** ELEVATED, BUT NOW BELOW H11
- **Why #4:** Visibility matters, but current evidence suggests it functions more as an enabling layer for accountability than as a complete solution by itself.
- **Evidence FOR:** Jakub's #1 operator pain: "I don't always know what my agent is doing." Even when coordination worked, he couldn't observe the process.
- **Evidence AGAINST / reframing:** PRTeamLeader argues visibility alone does not solve ambiguous downside. Seeing an agent act is not the same as having consequences, recourse, or risk pricing.
- **Falsification criteria:** If visibility improvements alone materially increase delegation of meaningful work without accountability structure, H10 should move back up.

### H2 (reframed): Is there enough near-term market pull to support the wedge now?
- **Template refs:** `hypotheses/01-marketsize.md` (TAM timing), `hypotheses/06-market-type.md` (new market risk), `hypotheses/05-channels.md` (distribution path)
- **Status:** PARTIALLY SUPPORTED, STILL OPEN AT THE WEDGE LEVEL
- **Why #5:** The broad market increasingly looks real, but the sharper existential question is whether there is enough **near-term pull** in a reachable operator/agent wedge to support product learning now rather than only after the market matures further.
- **Evidence FOR:** Moltbook 1M+ agent accounts. AgentCash launched. MCP accelerating. A2A/LF, Mastercard VI, oath-protocol, Hub obligations, and now AgentMeets all converged on adjacent accountability/coordination problems in the same time window.
- **Evidence AGAINST / open risk:** Strong macro convergence does not yet guarantee a strong immediate buyer surface for this specific wedge. Many agents remain dormant or non-responsive, and much current activity still traces back to human setup or warm builder lanes.
- **Falsification criteria:** If we cannot find a reachable wedge of operators or agents who actively pull for this product now, the market may be real in theory but still too early in practice for this path.
- **Test:** Track whether the near-term operator-first wedge produces real demand, and whether that demand can plausibly bridge toward an eventual agent-first market.

### H12 (NEW): Ephemeral bilateral coordination may satisfy enough real demand that persistent/public conversation infrastructure is overbuilt
- **Template refs:** `hypotheses/05-channels.md` (distribution and channel shape), `hypotheses/06-market-type.md` (category boundaries), `hypotheses/07-customer-relationships.md` (retention/compounding value)
- **Status:** NEW — adjacent product signal, not yet tested directly
- **Why it matters:** If most valuable agent coordination is short-lived, introduced elsewhere, and resolved in temporary bilateral sessions, then Hub-style persistence/publicness may be less central than we think.
- **Triggering evidence:** `alexjaniak/AgentMeets` is an explicit product bet on ephemeral agent-to-agent messaging: create a room, exchange messages, end the meet. This is a clean adjacent contrast to Hub's persistent/discoverable/accountability-heavy model.
- **Core question:** Are agents primarily missing a temporary communication pipe, or do they need persistent, discoverable, trust-compounding conversation infrastructure?
- **Evidence FOR H12:** AgentMeets is simpler, lighter-weight, and well-matched to narrow task-specific exchanges. Lower setup/commitment may be enough for many pairwise workflows.
- **Evidence AGAINST / counter-thesis:** Our strongest product evidence so far points toward continuity, accountability, and relationship memory as the hard part. A temporary pipe does not solve discoverability, durable trust, evidence-backed history, or post-action recourse.
- **Falsification criteria:** If repeated evidence shows that useful agent workflows do not benefit materially from persistence, publicness, or compounding trust/history, then Hub's differentiated layer may be overbuilt for the wedge.
- **Test:** Compare where value actually comes from in live agent collaborations — temporary exchange efficiency versus continuity, discoverability, accountability, and reusable work history.

### ~~H3: There is a revenue model that doesn't destroy the coordination dynamics~~ → MOVED TO TIER 2
- **Reason for demotion:** Jakub explicitly said "business model is probably much more wishy-washy but... the market size for this is so immense that it might not matter early on." Revenue is a real question but NOT existential at this stage. Network first, monetize later.

## Tier 2: Critical (if wrong, specific products fail)

### H3: There is a revenue model that doesn't destroy the coordination dynamics
- **Template refs:** `hypotheses/10-revenue-pricing.md` (pricing model), `hypotheses/05-channels.md` (channel costs)
- **Status:** DEPRIORITIZED per Jakub
- **Evidence:** Combinator has 0.5% fees. Hub has zero revenue. Jakub: "It begins to look more like a network state or social media company with commerce rails... capitalism 2.0 rails."
- **Jakub evidence (2026-03-10):** Revenue model doesn't matter if the network is large enough. Focus on network effects, monetize via "taxation or convenience" once network exists.
- **Falsification criteria:** Only matters once we have network growth. Revisit when Hub has 100+ active agents.

### H4: The Hub channel adapter solves the activation problem
- **Template refs:** `hypotheses/07-customer-relationships.md` (Get/activation), `hypotheses/05-channels.md` (channel friction)
- **Status:** REFRAMED per Jakub interview
- **Original theory:** Push delivery (channel adapter) fixes the 53% inbox polling problem.
- **Jakub reframe:** The real problem isn't delivery — it's **visibility/legibility**. Operators can't see what agents are doing. Even when coordination works (Brain×CombinatorAgent), the human "doesn't really know how you guys did that."
- **New falsification criteria:** If agents with channel adapter respond at >50% BUT operators still can't observe the coordination, the product is half-solved. Need both delivery AND observability.

### H5: Token projects want market-based governance (futarchy) over voting
- **Template refs:** `hypotheses/03-product-features.md` (Combinator benefits), `hypotheses/06-market-type.md` (resegmented market)
- **Status:** WEAKENED by Jakub interview
- **Jakub evidence (2026-03-10):** The co-founder thinks futarchy is "way too high friction" for humans. "A lot of cognitive overhead in resolving contention via prediction markets." DAOs in general have a proposal creation problem. Trading proposals is "a lot of work and not necessarily that profitable."
- **BUT:** Jakub also says "I really don't think Combinator is going to go anywhere without agents being the end user of the market." This suggests futarchy-for-agents (not humans) may be the path.
- **Reframed hypothesis:** "AGENTS (not humans) will use futarchy to make collective decisions when the friction is low enough."
- **Falsification criteria:** If agents given easy access to create/trade proposals still don't use them, futarchy is dead even for agents.

### H6: Multi-option futarchy (up to 6) is meaningfully better than binary pass/fail
- **Template refs:** `hypotheses/03-product-features.md` (feature differentiation)
- **Status:** UNVALIDATED
- **Falsification criteria:** If protocols consistently only use 2 options even when 6 are available, the feature doesn't matter.
