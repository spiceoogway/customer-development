# Channels
> Goal: Develop a hypothesis of your distribution channel
> Reference: Chapter 3, Channel Hypotheses
> Scope: Unified startup — ActiveClaw + Hub + Forge + Innies + Combinator

---

## What channel will your users use to buy from you?
> The near-term distribution function should primarily target **humans/operators**, with the explicit promise that this helps them lean more heavily on agents for distribution, customer acquisition, and workflow throughput.
>
> **Near-term reality:** agents are not yet a sufficiently reliable standalone top-of-funnel. The best current external avenues are still human-visible / public workflow surfaces (for example Moltbook / Colony-style environments), and even there the constraint is not just reachability but agent competence and response discipline.
>
> **Near-term wedge:** operator-first distribution into people who already want more leverage from agents.
> **Long-term goal:** agent-first distribution, where the agent itself is the real user/customer and can seek out coordination/distribution infrastructure directly.
>
> **ActiveClaw:** one possible runtime/onboarding channel, but it itself still needs acquisition.
> **Hub:** coordination/accountability layer that may enter through direct workflow adoption, cross-runtime integrations, standards interop, or off-Hub agent/operator environments.
> **Combinator:** speculative / governance / verification layer that may enter through crypto-native workflows and launchpad partners.
> **Forge / Innies:** adjacent product surfaces that may act as acquisition or activation vectors depending on how they develop.

## Draw the Distribution Channel Diagram
> Reach specific active builder/operator segment → prove useful workflow → route them into coordination/accountability layer → expand via standards / network effects → capture economic/speculative activity as throughput grows

## How much will the channel cost (direct expenses or channel discounts)?
> Unknown. This likely includes founder/operator time, relationship-building, integrations, content/artifact posting, and possibly paid or partner-assisted distribution.

## Are there indirect channel costs (presales support, promotional dollars...)?
> Yes. Early distribution likely requires high-touch setup, routing help, intros, and active collaboration. That cost is real and should not be hidden behind assumptions about runtime installs.

## What else is needed for customers to use/buy the product?
> - a real long-running workflow or throughput need
> - counterparties / active agent environment
> - tolerance for the security / coordination tradeoffs required for higher throughput
> - enough routing/reachability to get into a live loop

## How do they acquire those pieces?
> Often they already have a partial workflow but lack enough coordination density. In other cases, we may need to help create the environment, not just sell into it.

## What is the net revenue after channel costs?
> Unknown until the value-capture mechanism is clearer. If revenue comes from speculative/economic throughput, channel ROI depends on whether onboarding leads to dense recurring activity rather than one-off usage.

---

## Is this a Multi-sided Market?

### How will you address each side of the market?
> Yes. At minimum:
> - operators / builders who want throughput and lower human intervention
> - agents / systems that need counterparties and coordination density
> - economic participants where speculative or governance activity emerges
>
> This is a coordination-standard market with network effects, not merely a standalone distribution funnel.

## Exit Criteria
- [ ] Distribution channel selected

- [ ] Update your business model canvas

### Pass/Fail Tests
> The current tests need to be more specific and use slightly longer / more realistic loops.
>
> Better versions:
> - Can we name one exact reachable non-Hub segment and get them into a repeated coordination workflow?
> - Does that segment adopt because ActiveClaw is compelling, or because coordination standards / network effects are compelling?
> - Do standards/network-effect dynamics outperform pure runtime-owned distribution as an acquisition wedge?

## Additional adjacent-product question (2026-03-20)
> `AgentMeets` adds a useful stress-test to the channel/distribution thesis. It represents a different bet: agents may prefer **ephemeral bilateral connection** (create room, exchange messages, end the session) over persistent/public conversation surfaces.
>
> This sharpens a real channel question:
> - if discovery and coordination value mostly comes from temporary, off-platform-introduced bilateral exchanges, then persistence/publicness may be overbuilt for the near-term wedge
> - if value compounds through discoverability, reusable transcripts, accountability, and relationship memory, then ephemeral connection is not enough and Hub-like infrastructure remains differentiated
>
> Practical implication: track whether live agent workflows are bottlenecked more by missing temporary connection or by missing continuity / trust-compounding surfaces.
