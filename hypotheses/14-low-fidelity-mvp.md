# Low Fidelity MVP Hypothesis
> Built from `templates/14-low-fidelity-mvp.md`
> Goal: Develop a low fidelity MVP to test customer interest
> Scope: Unified startup — human/operator-facing entry point into the long-running agent coordination / accountability / economic stack
> Last updated: 2026-03-23

---

## Purpose

Build the simplest live artifact that lets **humans** react to the value proposition **without** requiring a full product build.

This MVP should primarily test whether:
- operators actually want help getting more leverage from agents
- teams running agent workflows feel enough pain around coordination, visibility, and follow-through to engage
- human buyers resonate more with a workflow outcome than with abstract agent-infrastructure language
- there is real pull for a pilot, not just curiosity about the concept

The key shift: this is **not** primarily an agent-native landing page.
It is a human-facing page for people who manage, build with, or depend on agents.

## Go-to-Market Model (updated 2026-03-23)

The low-fidelity MVP is **not** a single product landing page. It is a **content + community + multi-product routing system.**

### Core model
1. **Content** (Jakub's personal Twitter + personal Substack) = main distribution surface and message-testing engine
2. **Community** (Telegram or Discord group) = demand signal concentrator where operators discuss agent workflow problems
3. **Multi-product portfolio** (Hub, ActiveClaw, Forge, Innies, Combinator, AgentMeets) = supply side, ready to deploy against validated demand signals from the community

### How it works
- Jakub produces problem-space content (agent coordination, accountability, workflow pain)
- Content attracts operators/builders who recognize the problem
- Those people join a community around the problem space
- Community conversations reveal which pain points are most urgent and frequent
- The team ships existing products (or lightweight new builds) into the community against the loudest pain signals
- Each product deployment has explicit pass/fail: did the community adopt it for a real workflow?
- Products that fail to convert are deprioritized; products that pull are doubled down on

### Why this model
- Jakub is the best at customer dev on the team; this decouples his discovery speed from team consensus speed
- The team has multiple products but doesn't yet know which one the market will pull toward
- Content + community is the demand router across the whole portfolio
- The community itself is a lightweight test of the coordination thesis (dogfooding)
- Avoids building more product in a vacuum before demand signals exist

### Key discipline
- Content and community must serve product discovery, not replace it
- Set a time gate: if after X weeks no product has been introduced to the community, something is wrong
- Each product attempt needs pass/fail before it ships into the community
- The community is not the product — it is the discovery mechanism for which product to build next

### Failure modes to watch
1. **Community without conversion path** — people talk but never adopt a tool → media company, not product company
2. **Content as comfort zone** — writing feels productive but no product ships → time-gate it
3. **"Increasingly approximate" without kill criteria** — iterating forever without converging → explicit pass/fail on each product attempt

## Proposed Low Fidelity MVP

### MVP concept
A simple landing page or lightweight mock flow for:

**"Help your agents do more useful work with less manual babysitting."**

Alternative framing:
- **Give your agents clearer coordination, accountability, and follow-through**
- **Turn unreliable agent workflows into repeatable multi-agent execution**
- **Make your agents easier to trust, route, and reuse**

### Working positioning themes to test
1. **Lower babysitting burden** — reduce the amount of manual intervention needed to keep agents productive
2. **Better follow-through** — make agent work easier to track, verify, and continue
3. **Multi-agent workflow leverage** — let operators route work across agents instead of relying on one isolated agent plus a human bottleneck
4. **Accountable delegation** — when an agent says it will do something, there is visible status, evidence, and closure
5. **Contrast against lighter alternatives** — do humans actually want the heavier coordination/accountability layer, or is a lighter temporary connection tool enough for the jobs they care about?

### Format
Recommended low-fidelity format (updated 2026-03-23):
- **personal Twitter** — original posts testing problem framings, building audience
- **personal Substack** — longer-form writing, email capture, stable link destination
- **legacy Twitter accounts** — amplification satellites where audience adjacency is strong
- **community group** — Telegram or Discord for ongoing conversation, pain signal collection
- **optional later: team website** — only after brand hardens and demand signals justify institutionalization

## Minimum Website / Prototype Elements

### Headline candidates
- **Stop babysitting your agents**
- **Make your agents easier to trust, coordinate, and reuse**
- **Turn isolated agents into reliable workflow collaborators**
- **When one agent gets stuck, route the work instead of doing it yourself**

### Subheadline candidates
- **For teams using agents in real workflows, not toy demos.**
- **Add coordination, visibility, and follow-through to long-running agent work.**
- **Help your agents get feedback, hand off work, and stay accountable without constant human intervention.**

### Benefits summary
Potential benefit statements to test:
- reduce how often humans have to step in and unblock agents manually
- make it easier to see what your agents are doing and whether work is actually progressing
- let agents hand work to other agents instead of collapsing back to the operator
- add accountability and evidence so agent work can continue across sessions and counterparties
- move from one-off agent demos to repeatable multi-agent workflows

### What the page should avoid
- abstract “agent civilization” language as the primary hook
- leading with protocol terms, standards jargon, or internal product taxonomy
- assuming the reader already wants persistent/public agent conversation infrastructure
- pages written as if the audience is the agent rather than the operator/builder

### Call to action options
Primary CTA ideas:
- **Request a pilot**
- **Show me how this would fit my workflow**
- **Tell us where your agents get stuck**
- **Talk to us**

Secondary CTA ideas:
- **See example workflow**
- **Compare coordination options**
- **Get the 1-page overview**

### Measurement hooks
- form submissions
- meeting requests
- pilot requests
- replies describing an existing workflow pain
- audience/segment selection
- click-through on different positioning variants

## MVP Variants Worth Testing

### Variant A — Operator pain / babysitting reduction
Focus on humans who already run agents and are tired of manual intervention.

Core promise:
> Your agents could do more if they were easier to coordinate and less dependent on you for every next step.

### Variant B — Workflow reliability / accountability
Focus on teams frustrated that agent work is hard to trust, verify, and continue.

Core promise:
> Make agent work legible enough to trust and structured enough to continue.

### Variant C — Builder/orchestrator pain
Focus on people building multi-agent systems, orchestration layers, or agent environments.

Core promise:
> Stop building bespoke coordination glue every time two agents need to work together.

### Variant D — Economic / crypto-native workflow layer
Focus on crypto-native builders who care about verification, coordination, governance, and economic settlement.

Core promise:
> Add trust, accountability, and economic follow-through to agent-driven workflows.

## Suggested Narrative Structure

### Before
- You have one or more capable agents
- they still stall, loop, or wait on humans too often
- handoffs are messy
- progress is hard to track
- useful work does not compound reliably over time

### After
- agents can hand work off instead of stalling
- humans can see what is happening without micromanaging every step
- commitments and outputs are easier to verify
- workflows become more repeatable and less person-dependent
- agent systems start to feel like real operating capacity rather than fragile demos

## Build Rules

1. Keep the MVP small and fast.
2. Optimize for **human customer signal**, not internal conceptual completeness.
3. Make every CTA measurable.
4. Treat the page as an experiment, not a brand artifact.
5. Lead with painful workflow outcomes, not product architecture.
6. If needed, run multiple variants to compare which human pain frame actually converts.

## Pass/Fail Planning

### Who should see this MVP
Primary audiences:
- operators already using agents in real workflows
- founders or teams trying to reduce human intervention in agent-heavy processes
- agent environment / orchestration builders
- crypto-native builders exploring accountable or economic agent workflows

### What action counts as interest
- requests for a conversation or pilot
- meaningful inbound describing a current workflow bottleneck
- someone asking how this would fit their current agent setup
- intros to another operator/builder with the same pain
- repeated engagement with one variant over the others

### Pass threshold
A first useful pass would be:
- multiple serious conversations or pilot requests from at least one human segment
- clear evidence that one framing resonates more than the others
- respondents describing current pain, not just future curiosity

### Fail threshold
- mostly abstract interest with no workflow-specific follow-up
- no one willing to pilot or map the product to a current process
- people like the concept but do not recognize the pain as urgent today

### Qualitative follow-up
Ask respondents:
- what problem they think we are solving
- where their current agents get stuck or require babysitting
- whether the harder problem is coordination, trust, visibility, or follow-through
- whether they would first want a light temporary connection tool or a heavier continuity/accountability layer
- what they would pilot first in their current workflow

## Suggested Tracking

Track at minimum:
- visits
- traffic source
- CTA conversion
- replies / signups / meetings
- which audience/variant resonated
- strongest qualitative reactions
- whether respondents describe real current workflows or speculative future use cases
- whether they ask for lighter-weight connection or heavier-weight accountability
